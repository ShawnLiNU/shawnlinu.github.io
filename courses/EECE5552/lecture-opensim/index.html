Motivation

Why Can't We Just Measure Muscles Directly?

The Problem

  • Muscles are deep inside the body
  • We can measure joint angles & ground forces
  • We cannot directly measure individual muscle forces in vivo
  • Yet clinicians & engineers need to know them

Real-World Questions

  • Why does a patient walk with a crouch gait?
  • How much does an exoskeleton reduce biceps load?
  • Which muscles prevent ACL injury during landing?
  • Should a surgeon lengthen the hamstrings?

What is OpenSim?

What is OpenSim?

"A free, open-source platform for modeling the musculoskeletal system and simulating human and animal movement."
  • Developed at Stanford University (Delp Lab); maintained by the Mobilize Center (NIH)
  • Used by 50,000+ researchers, clinicians, and engineers worldwide
  • Models bones, joints, muscles, tendons, ligaments, and contact forces
  • Runs on Windows, macOS, and Linux — free at opensim.stanford.edu
  • Includes sample models: gait, arm, shoulder, spine, and more

What Does a Musculoskeletal Model Contain?

Body
Rigid Segments
Skull, pelvis, femur, tibia, humerus — each with mass and inertia
Joint
Joints
Connect bodies. Define degrees of freedom (e.g. elbow = 1-DOF hinge)
Muscle
Muscles & Tendons
Attach via path points. Generate forces based on activation, length, velocity
Marker
Markers
Virtual landmarks matched to motion capture data for model scaling
Act.
Actuators
Motors or idealized torques — used to model exoskeletons and assistive devices

Core Concepts

Key Concept 1: Degrees of Freedom (DOF)

A DOF is one independent direction in which a joint can move.

Elbow
1
Flexion / Extension
Knee
1
Flexion / Extension
Wrist
2
Flexion + Radial/Ulnar
Shoulder
3
Flexion, Abduction, Rotation
Hip
3
Flexion, Abduction, Rotation
The EduExo kit has exactly 1 DOF — it assists elbow flexion and extension only.

Core Concepts

Key Concept 2: Muscle Activation vs. Force

Activation (0 → 1)

  • Neural signal from brain to muscle
  • 0 = fully relaxed, 1 = max voluntary contraction
  • Measured experimentally via EMG
  • Estimated computationally via Static Optimization

Force (Newtons)

  • Actual mechanical pull the muscle exerts
  • Depends on: activation, fiber length, velocity
  • Same activation → different force at different angles
  • Modeled with Hill-type muscle model
Activation is the input; Force is the output. These are not interchangeable.

Core Concepts

Key Concept 3: The Analysis Pipeline

1
Scaling
Resize the generic model to match your subject's body dimensions.
IN: Generic model + static trial  |  OUT: Subject-specific scaled model
2
Inverse Kinematics (IK)
Find joint angles that best match the measured marker positions over time.
IN: Scaled model + motion capture (.trc)  |  OUT: Joint angle trajectories (.mot)
3
Inverse Dynamics (ID)
Calculate net joint torques required to produce the observed motion.
IN: Joint angles + ground reaction forces  |  OUT: Net joint moments (.sto)
4
Static Optimization (SO)
Distribute net joint moments across individual muscles — solves the muscle redundancy problem.
IN: Net moments + muscle properties  |  OUT: Per-muscle activations & forces
